True Luxury in Food Doesn’t Cost a Fortune

Manish MandalJune 15, 2019

I remember the first time I tasted saffron, in a risotto. It was earthy and weird, like rotten hay. But the person who made the risotto told me that saffron was special, because it was the most expensive spice in the world. So I ignored my own senses and decided the saffron must in fact be delicious. A food that cost so much couldn’t possibly taste bad, right?

This memory came back to me last week when I was in Barcelona listening to a brilliant talk by Alex Atala, who is widely regarded as the top chef in Brazil. The gist of Mr. Atala’s talk (part of a conference called “Fixing the Future”) was that we judge food far too much by its price tag and not enough by the pleasure it gives us or how sustainable it is. Mr. Atala—who looks like a biker, with a gingery beard and heavy tattoos—thinks that this is especially the case when it comes to so-called luxury ingredients.

Take caviar. Mr. Atala finds it ludicrous that rich diners in Brazil consider imported caviar to be a food of great value, but they don’t value South American delicacies such as tucupi. What’s tucupi? It is a deeply flavored yellow sauce made from cassava juice, lightly fermented and boiled down. Mr. Atala isn’t denying that caviar is delicious. His point is that if we think caviar is fancier than tucupi, it’s mainly because we have been told this is so. In an episode of the Netflix show “Chef’s Table,” he remarks that the true luxury in eating is how food makes us feel and the emotions and memories it provokes.

It’s not quite true to say that luxury is all in the mind. Some luxury foods are expensive simply because they are so difficult and labor-intensive to cultivate. Madagascan vanilla is a case in point. In recent years, as vanilla farms have been hit by tropical storms, the price has soared to around $600 a kilogram, which makes vanilla beans considerably more expensive by weight than solid silver.

But Mr. Atala is surely right to argue that chasing after luxury isn’t the best way to eat. At any given time, there are certain foods that have a higher status, but that doesn’t mean that they are truly more worthy of reverence than other, humbler ingredients. What’s more, our ideas of luxury are constantly changing. To the ancient Romans, the apple was a more luxurious fruit than the fig; in Victorian times, the oyster was a cheap snack rather than a deluxe dinner. And the chicken that we now eat by the bucket was once a rare and delicate extravagance.
